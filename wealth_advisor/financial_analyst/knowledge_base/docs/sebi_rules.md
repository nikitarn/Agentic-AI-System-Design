# SEBI Rules for Mutual Fund and Securities Investors

> Educational reference content, not registered investment advice.

## What SEBI Is

The Securities and Exchange Board of India (SEBI) is the regulator for the
securities market in India, established under the SEBI Act, 1992. It
registers and oversees mutual funds, brokers, and portfolio managers, and
issues the rules that asset management companies (AMCs) must follow when
launching and running mutual fund schemes — most notably the **SEBI (Mutual
Funds) Regulations, 1996**.

## Mutual Fund Scheme Categorization

SEBI's Categorization and Rationalization circular (SEBI/HO/IMD/DF3/CIR/P/2017/114,
Oct 6, 2017, later amended) requires every AMC to offer **at most one scheme
per category**, so investors can compare funds across houses on a like-for-like
basis instead of facing dozens of overlapping products with different names.
Schemes fall into five groups:

- **Equity schemes** — e.g. Large Cap, Large & Mid Cap, Mid Cap, Small Cap,
  Multi Cap, Flexi Cap, Focused, Dividend Yield, Value/Contra (an AMC may
  offer only one of the two), Sectoral/Thematic, ELSS
- **Debt schemes** — e.g. Overnight, Liquid, Ultra Short Duration, Low
  Duration, Money Market, Short/Medium/Long Duration, Corporate Bond, Credit
  Risk, Banking and PSU, Gilt, Dynamic Bond, Floater
- **Hybrid schemes** — e.g. Conservative Hybrid, Balanced/Aggressive Hybrid
  (an AMC may offer only one of the two), Dynamic Asset Allocation, Multi
  Asset Allocation, Arbitrage, Equity Savings
- **Solution-oriented schemes** — Retirement Fund, Children's Fund (both
  carry a mandatory lock-in)
- **Other schemes** — Index Funds/ETFs, Fund of Funds (FoF)

This is the categorization this knowledge base assumes when discussing fund
types elsewhere (see `mutual_funds.md`).

## The Riskometer

SEBI mandates that every scheme display a **Riskometer** — a visual dial
disclosing the scheme's risk level — in its scheme documents, fact sheets,
and any advertisement wherever the scheme name appears. Risk levels are
reviewed and updated periodically as market conditions change, and there are
six levels, in increasing order of risk:

1. **Low** — capital preservation focus, minimal volatility
2. **Low to Moderate** — slightly higher risk, still relatively stable
3. **Moderate** — a balanced risk/return profile
4. **Moderately High** — meaningful exposure to market volatility
5. **High** — higher risk than moderate, higher potential return
6. **Very High** — highly volatile, meant for aggressive investors

A scheme's Riskometer level is not a guarantee of return or of downside
protection — it is a disclosure tool, not a rating of quality.

## KYC Requirements

Under India's Prevention of Money Laundering Act, 2002, every mutual fund
and securities market investor must complete **Know Your Customer (KYC)**
verification before investing. In practice this means:

- **Identity + address proof** — PAN card is mandatory; address/identity can
  additionally be established via Aadhaar, passport, voter ID, or driving
  licence (utility bills are no longer accepted as address proof as of the
  2024–2025 KYC refresh cycle)
- **In-Person Verification (IPV)** — can now be completed digitally via video
  KYC or Aadhaar-based e-KYC rather than requiring a physical visit
- **KYC Registration Agencies (KRAs)** — once KYC is done through any one
  KRA (CVL, NSDL, CAMS, NSE/DotEx, or Karvy), it is valid across all AMCs and
  intermediaries — investors don't repeat the process per fund house

## Investor Protection Measures

Beyond categorization, the Riskometer, and KYC, SEBI's investor-protection
framework for mutual funds includes:

- **Concentration limits** — caps on single-stock, sector, and group-company
  exposure within a scheme, to limit concentration risk
- **Nomination** — AMCs must let investors (or joint holders) nominate who
  receives units on death
- **Disclosure** — offer documents must be complete and not misleading; SEBI
  can require changes to protect investors; portfolios must be disclosed
  periodically so investors can see what they actually hold
- **Advertisement Code** — fund advertisements must be submitted to SEBI
  within 7 days of release and must not make misleading or guaranteed-return
  claims
- **Grievance redressal** — AMCs must maintain a grievance mechanism, and
  investors can escalate unresolved complaints through SEBI's online
  grievance platform (SCORES)

## Why This Matters for Recommendations

Any recommendation this assistant makes should stay inside these guardrails:
cite the actual Riskometer level rather than inventing a risk assessment,
never state a return as guaranteed, and flag when a user's KYC or nomination
status might need attention rather than assuming it's already handled.
